Subtheme: Speculative Investment

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. What are different ways speculative investors make money?

    This question stems from a confusion I had, when I thought flipping was the only way investors made money and then saw this "conflicting information" in the executive summary:

    • "... investor activity is most likely to occur in the region’s high-density urban submarket with relatively low housing prices (Submarket 2)..."
    • "While flips occurred at similar rates across submarkets, we found flipping to be most common in Greater Boston’s highest value submarket, Submarket 5."

    This language seems to suggest there was high activity in Submarket 2 that was NOT flipping, and was likely purchasing cheap houses and making rent higher for the low-income population. I am curious to know what the actual breakdown of activity is (including what percentage of investment activity is flips), and am curious to know if the data comes aggregated by it.

  2. What are the profit margins of flipping different submarkets as a function of time?

    3. I am curious to know how investors gauge profitability, and why they tend towards suburban homes (Submarket 5) for flipping. Is it low risk high reward?

  3. How many houses are left unoccupied as a result of speculative investment?

    4. Out of personal experience: I have a friend that is quite successful in real-estate investments. She owns quite a few lands and houses around Asia, some of which are left unoccupied for most of the year. I wonder if there is a significant number of houses that are just always on Zillow but released by different owners.

  4. When will the housing prices go down? Will this scheme forever be profittable?

    5. The proposed solutions in the MAPC report are based on increased government regulation of real-estate transactions. If there are no such regualtions, what is the projected landscape of Boston in the next 20 years? Are we on a road to exponential growth of wealth inequality? Or will the bubble "burst" and are there any implications of this for independent home-owners and residents?

Glossary

  • speculative investment: buying of residential property for purposes of profiting rather than living
  • flipping: the act of selling a property for profit within two years of buying, one method of speculative investment
